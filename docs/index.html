How Artists Get Paid From Streaming

By Elio Quinton

It is commonly understood that artists make money when their music is played on streaming platforms like Spotify, Apple Music, Deezer, Pandora, Tidal, YouTube Music, and Amazon Music. But the mechanics of how those payments actually work is unknown to most of us, which may sometimes lead to some misconceptions. For example, artists do not get a fixed rate for every stream, e.g. $0.00026 per stream, or $0.0037 per stream.

Not all artists will necessarily receive the same payment for the same number of streams either.

Let’s dive in and break down some fundamental principles that dictate how artists get paid from music streaming.

Artist and Intermediaries

Let’s start by examining who are the players involved in getting the artist’s music online and making sure they receive the money they are due.

Artist recording royalties

A bit of clarification before we start. For every track on a streaming platform, a number of parties may be due a payment, such as the artist, composer, lyricist, publisher, record label etc.

In order to keep things simple, this article focuses only on the recording royalties due to the primary/featuring artist(s).

Getting Music Online

First things first: before any music can be played on streaming platforms it has to get there.

Artists do not deal with streaming platforms directly. Instead, they use a distributor who will then take care of getting their music to streaming platforms.

In the case that the artist is signed to a record label, the record label may take care of the distribution on behalf of the artist.

Collecting Royalties

When music is streamed, royalties will be due to the right holders. The term “right holders” designates whoever owns the right to the recording. For example it can be the artist themselves if they are independent, or the record label if the artist is signed to one.

The distributor and/or label will then collect the corresponding royalties from the streaming platforms and pay those who are due to receive them (or a share thereof), such as the artist.

Where does the money come from?

You may be wondering: Where does the royalties money come from in the first place? Essentially, the streaming platforms devote a share of their revenue to the payment of royalties.

Spotify example

Let’s use Spotify’s financials for the third quarter (Q3) of 2020 as an example. These circles represent all of Spotify’s users.

Spotify reports that premium and (active) freemium users represent about 45% and 55% of total users, respectively.

Subscriptions generated $1,790M while ads generated $185M.

This means that premium subscribers represent around 45% of Spotify users and generate about 90% of the total revenue, while the 55% of ad-supported users only generate about 10% of the revenue.

Looking at it from a different perspective: premium subscribers generate over 12 times more revenue than freemium users.

From revenue to royalties

First, the streaming platforms add up the revenue they generated (ads + subscriptions).

This dollars stack represents the total revenue.

This revenue is then split two ways: the streaming platforms keep one part for themselves and redistribute the rest as royalties.

Although the exact numbers are confidential, the figures often quoted in the press suggest that streaming platforms distribute around 65-70% of their revenue to music right owners as royalties.

Once the platform share is put away, the remaining dollars stack represents the royalties money pot.

It will be split between all tracks on the platform.

Royalties proportional to revenue

Since the streaming platforms pay out a percentage of their revenue in royalties, it means that the size of the royalties money pot to share is proportional to this revenue.

In other words, more revenue means bigger royalties pie to share.

The converse is also true... Less revenue means a smaller royalties pot.

Royalties pro-rata of streams

The royalties pot is split between all tracks on the platform, pro-rata of their proportion of streams.

For example, let’s take a track of a given artist. If the track represents 0.1% of the total streams on the platform, it will get 0.1% of the royalties.

If the proportion of total streams is larger, this track will be allocated more royalties accordingly.

For example if the track was to get 50% of the total streams, it would get 50% of the royalties pot.

Similarly, if the track was to get 30% of the total streams it would get 30% of the royalties pot. You get the idea.

Note one subtlety: because the share of royalties allocated to a track is equal to the proportion of total streams, what matters is the number of track streams with respect to the total number of streams. Not just the raw number of streams that the track got.

If the track streams remain constant and the total number of streams on the platform increases (i.e. the other tracks get more streams), the track’s share of stream would be smaller.

Conversely, if the track streams remain constant and the total number of streams on the platform decreases, the track’s share of stream would be larger.

Track royalties split

Now that the royalties for the track have been calculated, they may be split between all parties who are entitled a share for this track.

This is typically when the distributor and/or label may take their cut.

The general principle is that the distributor/label is granted a share of the track royalties to get paid in exchange for the services they provide to the artist.

The distributor/label share is normally defined in the contract they have with the artist. There are plenty of different options on the market for the artist to choose from depending on what they want or need, so the numbers will vary wildly from one case to the next.

Depending on the cases, the distributor/label may take a decent share.

Or a small one.

Some distributors even offer plans for which they take no royalties share — i.e. the artists keep 100%.

The artist gets the remaining share of royalties, shown in green.

Note that for simplicity we are only considering the case where there is a single artist/musician entitled royalties on the track.

In the general case there could be several (e.g. band members, producer) and each of them would be entitled a split of the artists share, with the split ratios defined in a contract between them all.

Closing words

Ultimately, the source of financial income for musical artists, direct or indirect, comes down to the fans engaging with their music. This can take many shapes, of which streaming is just one. If you are a music enthusiast, keep supporting artists, be it by subscribing to their social media channels and sharing with friends, getting a music streaming subscription, attending their shows, or buying merch!
